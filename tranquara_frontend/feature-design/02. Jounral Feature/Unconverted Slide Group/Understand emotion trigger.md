
## 🧩 **Lesson Collection: Understanding Emotional Triggers**

### **Lesson Title:**

**Why We Feel What We Feel**

### **Lesson Goal:**

Help users understand how emotions are triggered, why they sometimes feel inappropriate, and how emotional responses can be shaped.

---

### 🧠 **Slide 1 — Introduction (Doc Slide)**

**Title:** How Our Emotional Reactions Sometimes Go Wrong  
**Content:**  
There are three ways our emotional reactions can become inappropriate:

1. **Wrong intensity** – the emotion fits, but it’s too strong (e.g., overreacting).
    
2. **Wrong expression** – we feel the right emotion but express it poorly (e.g., staying silent while angry).
    
3. **Wrong emotion** – we feel something that doesn’t fit the situation at all.
    

🪞 _Learning point:_ Emotional regulation isn’t about stopping emotions — it’s about aligning them with reality.

---

### 💭 **Slide 2 — Prompt Slide**

**Title:** Reflection on Your Emotional Fit  
**Prompt:**  
“Think of a recent moment when your emotional reaction felt _too much_ or _not enough_. What triggered it? What might have been a better way to respond?”

---

### 📘 **Slide 3 — Doc Slide**

**Title:** Why We Feel Different Things in Similar Situations  
**Content:**  
Not all emotional triggers are universal.

- Some are **shared** — like fear during a crash.
    
- Others are **personal or cultural** — like fear of heights or embarrassment in public.  
    ✨ _Key Question:_ Can we change what triggers our emotions?  
    → Yes — through awareness and reflective practice, we can reshape emotional associations over time.
    

---

### 🔍 **Slide 5 — Doc Slide**

**Title:** How Much Do We Really Know About Emotions?  
**Content:**  
Emotions arise quickly, often before we’re aware of what caused them.  
Most studies rely on **self-reports**, meaning they’re filtered by memory and awareness.  
We rarely catch emotions _as they happen_ — only _after_ we notice them.

---

### 🧠 **Slide 6 — Doc Slide**

**Title:** What Are the Auto Appraisers Sensitive To?  
**Content:**  
Our **auto appraisers** are brain systems constantly scanning our surroundings for survival-relevant cues — the “emotion alert database.”

- **Core relational themes** (e.g., danger → fear, loss → sadness) activate emotions automatically.
    
- **Reflective appraisal** occurs when we consciously evaluate what’s happening.
    

**Example:**  
Losing your motorcycle key might first trigger fear (“I can’t get home”) but then move into conscious worry as you reflect.

---

### 💭 **Slide 7 — Prompt Slide**

**Title:** Spot Your Emotional Triggers  
**Prompt:**  
“What kinds of situations tend to activate strong emotions for you? Are there patterns or recurring themes?”

---

### 🧩 **Slide 8 — Doc Slide**

**Title:** How Triggers Are Stored in the Brain  
**Content:**  
Your brain constantly monitors your environment using _auto appraisers_, scanning for anything resembling what’s in your **emotion alert database** — shaped by:

- **Biology** (our ancestral past)
    
- **Personal experiences**
    

---

### 📚 **Slide 9 — Doc Slide**

**Title:** 8 Common Types of Emotion Triggers**  
**Content:**

1. Automatic appraisers (instinctive responses)
    
2. Reflective appraisal (thinking about the meaning of events)
    
3. Memory recall
    
4. Imagination
    
5. Talking about past emotions
    
6. Empathy (feeling with others)
    
7. Learning from others what to feel
    
8. Social norm violations
    
9. Acting out an emotion intentionally
    

---

### 🧠 **Slide 10 — Story Slide**

**Title:** The Snake Example (From Öhman’s Study)**  
**Content:**  
Even when we try to stay calm, ancient mechanisms can override intention.  
In an experiment, when a snake struck, even a determined researcher reflexively jumped backward — proof of our built-in emotional reflexes.

### 🌿 **Slide 12 — Summary & CTA**

**Title:** Key Takeaways

- Emotions arise automatically — but reflection helps us reshape them.
    
- Triggers can be changed through awareness and practice.
    
- Your emotional reactions are not “wrong” — they’re just messages to be understood.
